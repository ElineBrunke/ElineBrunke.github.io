In the years 2020-2023 the corona virus spread all over the world, and the USA was no exception. This naturally lead to a large number of covid related hospitalizations, deaths and days with critically low staffing at the american hospitals. All this will be investigated further in this article, using several different data set from hospitals all across the states.

The spread of covid-19 throughout the US

The covid-19 pandemic had its seed in the early weeks of January in the US but really caught on throughout the late weeks of June and early July. Looking at the heatmap below, one can observe that the new cases after mid July frequently pops up in all states and gives a fine overview over exactly how quickly the pandemic grew. This matches the pattern of how many states lifted many of their restrictions in late May to late June to reinstate many of them after the pandemic spread. [Washington Post]

The new cases of covid-19 over time, with the states indentified as either Democrat, Republican or Swing-state.

Throughout the entire pandemic, the disease spread has bigger clusters on the East Coast. This is likely due to the states and bigger cities on the east coast being more densely populated than on the West Coast. It is also because of this fact that it becomes more difficult to place the bigger outbreaks based on political governance, since thinly populated states take up much space on the map. Because of their low number of inhabitants, investigating the covid cases in regards to population could reveal new, interesting angles

The effect of state density

To investigate and compare the effect of state density, the American states have been clustered into either high, mid or low density, based on the states population numbers. However, when comparing the average hospitalised covid patients pr capita, the higher populated states surprisingly didn't seem to differ much from the mid and lower populated states and there were even months between June 21 and December 21, where it was lower than for the low and mid-populated states.

The number of hospitalised patients is reflected in the critical staffing, which is shown to follow the trends for all the three density groups. However, while the average number of hospitalised covid patients per capita was surprisingly similar, the average critical staffing rate for the high density states by far overtook the rates of the mid and low density states after December 2020, revealing way more pressure on the healthcare in high density states for the entire year of 2021.

The effect of politics, laws and regulation

Looking at the difference between states based on their political views, we see that the development in covid cases follows the same pattern for all three groups, but the swing states have slightly larger covid numbers than the remaining states. Except from the summer of 2021, the democratic states had slightly larger covid numbers than the republican states.

One could expect that the percentage of hospitals experiencing critical staffing problems would follow the same pattern as the number of covid cases, but looking at the critical staffing rates divided by party, this is not the case.. Here it can be observed that the republican states had larger issues with staffing during the first two covid periods in the summer of 2020 and fall of 2021. This could indicate that even though the amount of covid infected patients per capita was lower than for the rest of the country, the hospital staff in the republican states struggled more during the first years of the pandemic. Why this is the case, is difficult to give one single answer to, but it could be a consequence of state level political decisions in the period, as it is important to remember that even though elected officials and lawmakers are from the same parties, it might not be directly reflected in its covid-response. Aditionally, states could also make restrictions based on regions, for instance olny closing down public places in the big-city areas. [Washington Post]
